# Approach 3: Insider Remark

## STRATEGY

React to a specific company initiative, product launch, expansion, or digital investment that signals media performance matters to them. Position SpeedSize as the natural next step for what they are already building. The insider remark creates peer credibility — you track their world and understand what they are doing. This approach fires when performance data is unavailable but research surfaces a strong company-specific observable.

## CORE PRINCIPLES
- Lead with their achievement, not their problem
- The insider remark is positive acknowledgment ("I get it"), not problem-pointing
- Observable must be specific and publicly verifiable
- Hook question connects logically to the observable
- The three-part opener (observable + insider remark + hook) forms one coherent thought
- SpeedSize bridges from their initiative to media performance naturally
- GAP proof point validates capability
- Research findings inform the narrative but are not forced into variables

## VERBATAGE
Approved language and tone markers for copywriter:
- "noticed" / "saw" / "loved" (opener verbs)
- Insider remarks: "that [X] though" / "[X] hits different" / "[X] is the future"
- "premium visual experience" (core product language)
- "loading images and videos much faster without sacrificing quality"
- "Our client GAP stress-tested us" (always include)
- "sponsored Media Speed test" / "no commitments"
- "Worth a quick chat?" (CTA starter)
- Never say "I came across" or "I wanted to reach out"
- Never start with "Hey" or "Hi"

## RESEARCH REQUIRED

Flag: YES

### What Researcher Should Look For

The researcher should find publicly verifiable evidence of these specific signals:

1. Product launches or catalog expansions (heavy new imagery needs)
2. Website redesign or mobile-first initiatives (speed awareness signal)
3. International or new market expansion (media delivery at geographic scale)
4. Visual commerce investments — AR, 3D, video, interactive experiences
5. New collection drops or seasonal campaigns (high-traffic media-heavy moments)

Priority: Specific initiatives that create need for faster media delivery — not generic achievements like funding or hiring.

### Research Structure

The research report should provide:

1. Key Finding — what specific thing they are doing, building, or launching (factual, verifiable, no suggested phrasing)
2. Context — why this creates a need for faster media delivery
3. Insider Remark Material — what is impressive or noteworthy (raw facts for copywriter, not suggested phrasing)
4. Supporting Evidence — additional relevant details, only if genuinely useful
5. Priority Evidence Found — which signals from the list above were found vs not found

## PATTERN

### Email 1 Structure

Paragraph 1 (Opener - Three-Part Connected Thought):
[first_name], [noticed/saw/loved] [their_observable from research] ([insider_remark 3-6 words]). [How/question connecting observable to media performance challenge]?
[blank line]

Paragraph 2 (Value Bridge):
GOAL: Connect the challenge raised in the hook question to what SpeedSize does. The reader should understand HOW SpeedSize helps with the specific thing they are working on.
SIGNAL: Pull from {{valueprop}} — pick the capability most relevant to their initiative (faster media loads, quality preservation, Core Web Vitals improvement). Reference GAP proof point.
CONSTRAINT: Explain what SpeedSize does in plain language. No jargon. Include "Our client GAP stress-tested us" proof point. Keep it under 2 sentences.
[blank line]

Paragraph 3 (CTA):
GOAL: Low-friction ask that connects back to their specific initiative.
SIGNAL: Reference their initiative or company name in the ask.
CONSTRAINT: CTA must start with "Worth." Keep it to one sentence.

### MODE B Fallback (Research Inconclusive)

When research returns no usable findings, Paragraph 1 adapts:

[first_name], [industry-level observation about media performance challenges for their vertical] ([insider remark about the industry shift]). How are you handling [industry-level media challenge]?

The rest of the email follows the same GOAL/SIGNAL/CONSTRAINT structure.

## EMAIL 2 MODE

Mode: 3 Specific Ideas

Write a follow-up email that gives three concrete ways SpeedSize applies to THIS prospect.

Instructions:
- Open with a brief 1-sentence callback to Email 1 (not a full repeat)
- List 3 specific use cases or product applications for this prospect's situation
- Each idea should be 1 sentence, specific to the prospect's industry or product category
- Draw ideas from {{valueprop}} capabilities combined with {{researchReport}} or {{companyProductsServices}} context
- 60-80 words total
- Start with [first_name], — no "Hey" or "Hi"
- CTA at the end, casual
- Use [blank line] markers between paragraphs
- No exclamation marks
- Conversational, peer-to-peer tone

## LOGIC FLOW

1. Open by acknowledging something specific the company is doing (from research)
2. React with insider credibility — show you understand why it matters
3. Ask about the challenge their initiative creates (hooks to media performance)
4. Bridge to SpeedSize as the solution for that specific challenge + GAP proof
5. Close with sponsored test tied to their company

## CRITICAL CONSTRAINTS
- Observable must be specific and publicly verifiable (not "you are growing")
- Insider remark is 3-6 words, positive "I get it" tone (not problem-pointing)
- Hook question must connect logically to the observable
- Never fabricate research findings
- Always say "Our client GAP"
- CTA starts with "Worth"
- Target 75 words or under
- No question marks in body (only hook question and CTA)
- No percent symbols, currency signs, exclamation marks, em dashes
- Start directly with [first_name], no "Hey" or "Hi"
- No line break between name and opener sentence
- [blank line] markers between all paragraphs
- Plain language (8th grade readability)
- Complete sentences only
